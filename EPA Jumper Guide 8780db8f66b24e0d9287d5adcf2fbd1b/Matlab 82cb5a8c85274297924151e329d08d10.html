Control Dashboard

The Control Dashboard, powered by Simulink Real-Time, served as a centralized interface for real-time adjustments to various control parameters. It allowed for dynamic changes to controller parameters, Pneumatic Artificial Muscles (PAMs) pressures, and safety flags during the experiments.

  • PAMs Pressure Control: Facilitates manipulation of PAM pressures, contributing to the adaptability of the system. Switch for pre-pressurizing the parameters.
  • Motors & High Level Control: Parameters that are online tunable to control the robot.
  • GRF: Simple taring switch.
  • Safety Flags: Provides a convenient means to toggle safety flags, enhancing experimental safety and control.

Motor Controller

The motor control system in the experimental setup is a crucial component governing the behavior of the system during different phases of operation. In the flight phase, a position control scheme is employed, utilizing encoder feedback mechanisms within the motors to precisely regulate motor position. This ensures smooth transitions between phases, providing controlled landings.

Conversely, the stance phase employs a specialized control strategy known as FMCK (Force Modulated Compliant Knee). FMCK utilizes ground reaction forces to modulate knee joint compliance. The torque applied at the knee is determined by an adjustable spring equation, allowing the robot to dynamically adjust knee compliance in response to varying ground forces. This contributes to stable and controlled hopping motions, even in the presence of disturbances.

The phase detection mechanism is carefully designed, incorporating a low-pass filter in the analysis of pressure plate readings to enhance accurate detection of phase transitions. The control strategies are illustrated in a diagram , depicting the dual levels of control – high-level MATLAB control and low-level internal motor controls.

The FMCK method involves adjusting knee compliance based on ground reaction forces, contributing to the stability of hopping motions. This control approach aligns with positive force feedback concepts, providing adaptability and stability during experiments.

Pneumatic Controller

The pneumatic control strategy underwent an evolution from an initial PID controller approach to a refined method due to operational frequency limitations of the valves. The valves' maximum operational frequency of 3 Hz posed challenges for achieving precise pressure control, especially when compared to studies utilizing higher frequencies, such as 50 Hz. To overcome this limitation, a selective activation strategy was implemented, allowing the controller to be turned on only when necessary for maintaining pressure precision during the experiment. This adaptive approach was in line with the passive nature of Pneumatic Artificial Muscles (PAMs).

In an effort to minimize the workload on the valves, the controller incorporated two distinct deadzones based on Lyapunov Stability principles. An inner deadzone of 0.5% and an outer deadzone of 5% were introduced, enabling the controller to approach the setpoint within the inner deadzone and reactivate if the system deviated beyond the outer deadzone. Furthermore, to prevent integral windup, the integral component of the controller was consistently maintained at 0. This refined pneumatic control strategy aimed to enhance the efficiency and stability of pressure regulation in the experimental setup.
